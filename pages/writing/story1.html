The Tides, or, The Shore Skull

The Skull sat alone on the shore, unperturbed.

It was used to its life, sat on a ridge in the sand, surrounded by the hardy, scraggly grass that grows in such places in spite of the wind and the salt-choked marsh below. The tide retreated, and the marsh dried out, the tall reeds swayed, and the fiddler crabs which occupied those grey lands in droves skittered about, re-excavating their homes and posturing at the salt-wind. Soon the tide would return, and sweep bare the land again. The cycle continued.

It was the precipice of fall now, and the breeze wavered between welcome relief and biting cold. The water, which was still a sparkling blue, was soon to be greyed by the clouds that came to roll in. Overhead a great flock of birds flew south to the lands beyond maps where they would over-winter. Inland, the settlers were engaged in a great, last-minute chopping-down of many of the poor, hardy trees that, like the grass, had fought hard to secure their place on these oft-violent shores.

But the Skull didn't have to worry about that anymore. The rest of the bones that it had once led in tree-chopping, oyster farming, feasting -all manner of hard work and merry making alike- had long since left on the tide, not long after its flesh had been wrested from it on that quick and violent night.

It was somehow secured against waves and storms like the grass and the trees. It stayed while the birds came and went. It had sunk deeper into the sand, but that was all the change that touched it. It saw the sun rise every morn', set every night. It saw the great white sails of the settler's ships that pulled into the harbor just out of sight.

Treasure, it thought when it would see them leave, that's what had drawn them here. But now the Skull was content to watch the plovers pluck their way across the waterline, little balls of fluff that dared the sea for its little morsels; treasure of their own.

The sun set orangely.

It rose red on a high tide.

The Skull watched. It had grown uneasy, watching light creep into the world that morning, sickly red. It was the anniversary of that night, it could tell. It wondered what would happen. Most things that the ocean spit out were reclaimed within a day, a week. The largest driftwood was a rare exception, if it got tossed up high by a storm.

The Skull had been there on this beach for a year.

That evening the tide retreated from another high, and a man appeared with a small wooden bucket. He walked along the dune, and looked through the marshy water for the large crabs that sometimes shared the space with the fiddlers.

The air buzzed, and not only with the sonorous songs of the choir of crickets.

The man paused on the ledge of sand, and looked around. Some bubbles rose in a pool next to the Skull, a pool that was oh so deep. The man saw the shadow of a massive crab within it, and with glee slid-shuffled his way down the sandy slope into the divot of the pool. He set down his bucket. He crouched, hands outstretched, ready to shoot into the water and claim his prize.

The Skull couldn't quite see the crabber anymore, but it knew. It could sense him. It could sense It. The man shot his hand into the water, the Skull could hear the splash, the brief “huzzah!” of a catch. Then there was another thrum.

There was a plunk as the man dropped the crab into the bucket.

The Skull saw the man start to leave, then yet another thrum called out, stronger this time. The man saw another crab, and started back into the divot.

Another thrum.

The Skull couldn't look away. It wanted to call out, but couldn't do that either.

A great darkness rose up out of the pool, and the man didn't see until it was too late. Great shadowy tentacles, which seemed to shift in the orange light, grabbed him. He flailed his arms, then he disappeared from the Skull's view. Then there was a gurgle —then silence.

The sun was red, and a great bank of clouds rolled in. On the horizon there formed a macabre jack-o-lantern grin made of sun and cloud on the edge of the sea. The Skull saw it and was sad. When the sun disappeared below the waves, there was a wet belch from the pool and a skeleton flew into the air and landed on the shore in a forlorn heap. The next morning the tide took it away, and the lone Skull was left behind on its ledge, and would continue to be. It didn't know why it stayed and the others left, why it was alone year after year.

Perhaps the Creature thought it funny.

Perhaps it thought it was some strange gift, a life on the oh-so-beautiful beach.

Perhaps It was lonely.
